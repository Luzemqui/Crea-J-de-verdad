Know, Detect, Prevent

Key information about the five most common types of cancer. Early detection saves lives.

Breast Cervix Próstate Lung

Breast cancer

What is it?

It originates when breast tissue cells grow uncontrollably, forming a tumor. It can occur in both men and women, although it is much more common in women.

Risk factors

  • Age over 40 years
  • Family history or genetic mutations (BRCA1/BRCA2)
  • Early onset of menstruation or late menopause
  • Overweight, a sedentary lifestyle, and alcohol consumption
  • Long-term hormone therapy

Warning signs

  • Lump or thickening in the breast or armpit
  • Changes in breast size, shape, or skin
  • Abnormal nipple discharge
  • Skin indentation or redness

Detection and prevention

Monthly self-examination, annual mammogram starting at age 40 (or earlier, depending on medical history), and clinical breast examination during routine medical check-ups.

Cervical cancer

What is it?

It develops in the cells of the cervix. It is strongly associated with persistent Human Papillomavirus (HPV) infections.

Risk factors

  • Persistent high-risk HPV infection
  • Early onset of sexual activity or multiple partners
  • Smoking
  • Weakened immune system
  • Not having been vaccinated against HPV

Warning signs

  • Abnormal vaginal bleeding (between periods, after intercourse, or during menopause)
  • Unusual vaginal discharge
  • Pelvic pain or pain during sexual intercourse
  • In the early stages, there may be no symptoms.

Detection and prevention

Pap smear (cytology) every 1–3 years depending on age and risk, HPV testing, and HPV vaccination for adolescents before the onset of sexual activity.

Prostate cancer

What is it?

It is the abnormal growth of cells in the prostate, a gland of the male reproductive system. It typically grows slowly and is often detected in the early stages.

Risk factors

  • Age over 50 years
  • Family history of prostate cancer
  • Afro-descendant ancestry
  • High-fat, low-vegetable diet
  • Obesity

Síntomas de alerta

  • Difficulty or pain when urinating
  • Weak or interrupted urine flow
  • Blood in the urine or semen
  • Persistent lower back, pelvic, or hip pain

Detection and prevention

Blood test for prostate-specific antigen (PSA) and a digital rectal exam starting at age 50, or at age 45 if there is a family history. Consult a doctor regarding any persistent urinary symptoms.

Lung cancer

¿What is it?

It originates when lung cells grow uncontrollably. It is one of the leading causes of cancer death worldwide, largely linked to smoking.

Risk factors

  • Active or passive smoking (the main factor)
  • Exposure to radon, asbestos, or air pollution
  • Family history
  • Occupational exposure to industrial chemicals

Warning signs

  • Persistent or worsening cough
  • Coughing up blood
  • Difficulty breathing or chest pain
  • Unexplained weight loss and fatigue

Detection and prevention

Quitting smoking is the most effective measure. Annual low-dose CT scanning is recommended for high-risk individuals (smokers or former smokers aged 50–80).

Early detection is the best tool against cancer. Always consult a healthcare professional.
