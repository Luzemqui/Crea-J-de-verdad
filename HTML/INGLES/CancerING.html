Know, Detect, Prevent

A detailed medical guide to the most commonly diagnosed types of cancer worldwide. Education and prevention are our best allies for health.

Breast cancer

What is it?

Breast cancer is a disease in which cells in the breast tissue mutate and multiply uncontrollably, typically resulting in a malignant tumor. It can originate in either the milk ducts (ductal carcinoma, the most common type) or the glandular lobules (lobular carcinoma). Although it primarily affects women from their forties onwards, men can also develop it, albeit in a smaller percentage of cases (approximately 1%).

Risk factors
  • Advanced age: The risk increases significantly from the age of 40 to 50.
  • Advanced age: Presence of inherited mutations in the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes, or a direct family history of breast or ovarian cancer.
  • Hormonal and reproductive factors: Early menarche (before age 12) or late menopause (after age 55), as well as prolonged exposure to hormone replacement therapy.
  • Lifestyle: Frequent alcohol consumption, being overweight or obese after menopause, and a sedentary lifestyle.
  • Radiological factors: History of radiation therapy treatments to the chest area during youth.
Warning signs
  • Presence of a palpable lump, mass, or thickening in the breast or underarm area—usually painless but firm.
  • Noticeable alterations in the morphology, size, or symmetry of the breasts.
  • Changes to the skin covering the breast, such as dimpling, puckering, an "orange-peel" appearance, or unexplained redness.
  • Nipple abnormalities, such as recent retraction (inversion), spontaneous discharge (especially if bloody), or persistent scaling.
Detection and prevention

The comprehensive early detection strategy includes monthly breast self-examinations starting at age 20 to become familiar with normal breast structure, annual professional clinical examinations, and periodic mammograms beginning at age 40. Recommended primary prevention measures include maintaining a healthy body weight, engaging in consistent physical activity, limiting or avoiding alcohol consumption, and prioritizing a diet rich in antioxidants and fiber.

Cervical cancer

What is it?

Also known as cervical cancer, it originates in the cells of the cervix (the lower part connecting to the vagina). Its development is typically very slow, beginning with precancerous lesions known as dysplasia. It is directly and almost exclusively linked to persistent infection with oncogenic types of the Human Papillomavirus (HPV), with genotypes 16 and 18 responsible for the majority of severe cases.

  • Depressed immune system: Conditions such as HIV/AIDS or the prolonged use of immunosuppressive medications.
  • Multiparity: Having had multiple full-term pregnancies without frequent gynecological check-ups.
Warning signs
  • Abnormal and unexpected vaginal bleeding, such as bleeding after sexual intercourse, bleeding between regular menstrual periods, or postmenopausal bleeding.
  • Unusual vaginal discharge, which may become persistent, watery, or thick, or show traces of blood and a foul odor.
  • Constant pelvic pain, discomfort, or a sensation of heaviness in the lower abdomen.
  • Discomfort or sharp pain during sexual intercourse (dyspareunia).
Detection and prevention

The Pap test (vaginal cytology) and molecular tests for detecting HPV DNA are highly effective tools capable of identifying lesions before they become malignant. Primary prevention includes preventive HPV vaccination for preadolescents before the onset of sexual activity, the correct and consistent use of barrier methods (condoms), and the reduction of tobacco use.

Prostate cancer

What is it?

Prostate cancer develops in the male prostate gland—a small, walnut-shaped organ located below the bladder whose primary function is to produce the seminal fluid that nourishes and transports sperm. It is one of the most frequently diagnosed tumors among men. The vast majority of cases are adenocarcinomas characterized by extremely slow progression, which allows for a very favorable prognosis if diagnosed early.

Risk factors
  • Advancing age: Incidence increases exponentially from the age of 50.
  • Family history: Having a father or brother diagnosed with prostate cancer doubles or triples one's personal risk.
  • Ethnic origin: Men of African descent exhibit a notably higher incidence and aggressiveness rate compared to other ethnic groups.
  • Diet and metabolism: Dietary patterns high in animal saturated fats and low in fruits, vegetables, and lycopenes, combined with a sedentary lifestyle.
Warning symptoms
  • Frequent urinary problems, especially a slow or weak urine stream, or a stream that stops and starts intermittently.
  • An urgent and sudden need to get up multiple times during the night to empty the bladder (nocturia).
  • Presence of traces of blood in both urine and semen.
  • Persistent discomfort, stiffness, or localized pain in the lower back, hips, or upper thighs due to advanced-stage bone metastases.
Detection and prevention

Preventive and early diagnostic methods rely on annual medical evaluations involving a Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) blood test and a digital rectal examination, starting at age 50 (or age 45 if there is a significant family history). It is recommended to maintain a balanced diet rich in vegetables, cruciferous vegetables, and tomatoes; to keep a healthy body weight; and to undergo regular medical check-ups if any urinary abnormalities arise.

Lung cancer

What is it?

Lung cancer originates in lung tissues, typically in the cells lining the airways and bronchi. It is primarily classified into two major groups: small-cell lung cancer and non-small-cell lung cancer. It is a leading cause of cancer-related mortality worldwide, with the overwhelming majority of cases strongly associated with prolonged tobacco use and the inhalation of smoke and its byproducts.

Risk factors
  • Active tobacco use: Responsible for more than 85% of total cases due to the massive presence of carcinogenic chemical agents in the smoke.
  • Passive smoking: Constant inhalation of environmental tobacco smoke generated by nearby smokers at home or work.
  • Radon gas exposure: A naturally occurring radioactive gas that accumulates indoors and in basements, originating from the soil and rock.
  • Occupational and industrial risks: Chronic inhalation of toxic substances such as asbestos, arsenic, beryllium, nickel, soot, and diesel emissions.
  • Air pollution: Exposure to high levels of fine airborne particles in large industrial cities.
Warning signs
  • A chronic cough that persists, changes in tone, or progressively worsens without an apparent infectious cause.
  • Coughing up blood or reddish-tinged sputum.
  • A constant sensation of shortness of breath (dyspnea) or breathlessness, the onset of wheezing, and sharp chest pain when breathing deeply.
  • Rapid, unexplained weight loss, chronic fatigue, generalized weakness, and recurrent episodes of pneumonia or bronchitis.
Detection and prevention

The single most effective preventive measure is to completely avoid tobacco use and quit smoking early. For individuals with a high risk profile related to tobacco use (adults aged 50 to 80 with a long history of smoking), medical protocols recommend annual preventive screening using low-dose computed tomography (LDCT), which allows for the identification of nodules at a very early stage.

Stomach cancer

What is it?

Gastric (or stomach) cancer develops when the cells lining the stomach mucosa begin to multiply abnormally, eventually forming a tumor. Its progression is typically insidious and slow over the course of years, usually preceded by chronic inflammatory processes such as atrophic gastritis. The predominant histological type is gastric adenocarcinoma, which responds favorably to combination treatments if detected before invading deeper layers or nearby lymph nodes.

Risk factors
  • Helicobacter pyloriinfection: Chronic bacterial colonization of the stomach that damages the mucosa and promotes long-term cellular mutations.
  • Dietary habits: Frequent consumption of foods preserved via heavy salting or smoking, as well as processed meats, combined with low intake of fresh fruits or vegetables rich in vitamin C.
  • Active smoking: Smoke and its ingested toxins substantially increase the risk of developing tumors in the gastric region.
  • Genetic background: Family history of gastric cancer or the presence of hereditary cancer-predisposition syndromes.
  • Pre-existing conditions: Chronic atrophic gastritis, pernicious anemia, or prior stomach surgery for benign ulcers.
Warning signs
  • Persistent indigestion, vague abdominal discomfort, chronic heartburn, or a constant burning sensation in the upper abdomen.
  • A feeling of fullness or early satiety after consuming very small portions of food.
  • Sudden weight loss, loss of appetite, and a sudden aversion to certain foods (such as red meat).
  • Frequent episodes of nausea, recurrent vomiting, and the presence of dark stools (melena) or vomit containing traces of blood.
Detection and prevention

Prevention focuses on the timely medical eradication of the Helicobacter pylori bacterium through specific antibiotic treatments upon detection, the adoption of a diet rich in fresh produce, a strict reduction in preservatives and salt, and the avoidance of smoking. In patients at high symptomatic risk or with a direct family history, performing an upper gastrointestinal endoscopy enables an accurate and early diagnosis.

Early detection is the best tool against cancer. Always consult a healthcare professional.
